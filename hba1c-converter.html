What is HbA1c, Why eAG Matters, and Global Diabetes Standards

What is HbA1c? HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin) measures what percentage of your hemoglobin — the oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells — has glucose attached. Since red cells live ~90-120 days, HbA1c reflects your average blood sugar over past 2-3 months, unlike a single fingerstick. It is the gold standard lab monitor used worldwide for prediabetes screening and diabetes management.

Why does eAG matter for daily tracking? HbA1c % alone is abstract. The eAG calculator (estimated Average Glucose) translates it via the ADAG formula eAG mg/dL = 28.7 × A1c − 46.7 into the same units you see on your glucometer or CGM. Then mmol/L = mg/dL ÷ 18 makes it a universal diabetic lab report translator. If your lab says A1c 7.2% (155 mg/dL / 8.6 mmol/L), but your meter average is 130 mg/dL, you know to check anemia, recent highs/lows, or CGM gaps. eAG bridges lab and life — linking A1c to A1c to average glucose mmol/L targets and Time-in-Range goals (70% between 70-180 mg/dL = 3.9-10 mmol/L).

Global diabetes standards: Most labs globally report HbA1c as NGSP % and IFCC mmol/mol (e.g., 7% = 53 mmol/mol). ADA defines Normal <5.7% (39 mmol/mol), Prediabetes 5.7-6.4% (39-47 mmol/mol), Diabetes ≥6.5% (≥48 mmol/mol). For tight control, aim per your endocrinologist — general ADA adult goal is A1c <7% (154 mg/dL / 8.6 mmol/L), <6.5% if low hypoglycemia risk, <8% if limited life expectancy or high comorbidities.

FAQ • HbA1c eAG
Formula for HbA1c to eAG?

eAG mg/dL = 28.7×A1c −46.7. Divide by 18 for mmol/L. Example 8% → 183 mg/dL → 10.2 mmol/L.

Normal vs diabetes A1c?

Normal <5.7% (117 mg/dL), Prediabetes 5.7–6.4% (117–139), Diabetes ≥6.5% (≥140 mg/dL / 7.8 mmol/L).

mg/dL vs mmol/L?

US uses mg/dL, rest of world mmol/L. mmol/L = mg/dL /18. This translator shows both for global reports.
